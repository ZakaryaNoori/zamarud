1. The Badakhshan province offers the bluest Lapis Lazuli on earth.

Lapis Lazuli is a blue rock made of several different minerals including lazurite, sodalite, hauyne, calcite (which shows as white flecks), and pyrite (which adds a golden yellow sparkle). The composition and color of each stone varies, but it's the general intense dark blue color that is considered its best quality.

Lapis lazuli has a uniform, massive appearance with distinct crystals, and can be semi opaque or opaque. Its color is of a strong, lively blue, sometimes with hint of violet. It often contains grayish or off-white patches or veins. The presence of these white patches reduces the value of the stone.

The most highly prized lapis lazuli stones are uniformly colored and without a violet tinge. Lapis lazuli stones vary in hardness, ranging from a 5 to 5.5 on the hardness scale. The stone has vitreous luster. Lapis Lazuli is found as boulders within limestone. It has been mined in Afghanistan since remote antiquity. In fact, ancient Egyptians probably obtained their supplies from there. Argentina is also a source of lapis lazuli but Afghanistan is the best high quality stones mines.

3. We cut for beauty, not weight

Most of the cultures have found uses of this beautiful Lapis Lazuli stone throughout the history. Besides the jewelry and medicinal, many uses were simply practical. Artisans have made jewelry out of lapis lazuli. It can be polished to a high shine for beads, pendants, bracelets, necklace, earing and rings.

This stone has also been popular for sculpture. It has added a bright blue to everything from small carvings to the eyes of statues.

Lapis lazuli also used to product a powdered pigment, and this pigment can be used as eye shadow, ultramarine paint, and dye.

Societies that used lapis lazuli for dye produced rich, deep blue cloth. Only if you were among the highest ranking citizens would you have been allowed to wear garments dyed with this pigment.
